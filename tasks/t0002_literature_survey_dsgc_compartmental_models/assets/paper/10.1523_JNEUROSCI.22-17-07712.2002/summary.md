# Diverse Synaptic Mechanisms Generate Direction Selectivity in the Rabbit Retina

## Metadata

* **File**: `files/taylor_2002_ei-ds-rabbit.pdf`
* **Published**: 2002
* **Authors**: W. Rowland Taylor 🇦🇺, David I. Vaney 🇦🇺
* **Venue**: The Journal of Neuroscience, 22(17):7712–7720
* **DOI**: `10.1523/JNEUROSCI.22-17-07712.2002`

## Abstract

The synaptic conductance of the On-Off direction-selective ganglion cells was measured during visual
stimulation to determine whether the direction selectivity is a property of the circuitry
presynaptic to the ganglion cells or is generated by postsynaptic interaction of excitatory and
inhibitory inputs. Three synaptic asymmetries were identified that contribute to the generation of
direction-selective responses: (1) a presynaptic mechanism producing stronger excitation in the
preferred direction, (2) a presynaptic mechanism producing stronger inhibition in the opposite
direction, and (3) postsynaptic interaction of excitation with spatially offset inhibition. Although
the on- and off-responses showed the same directional tuning, the off-response was generated by all
three mechanisms, whereas the on-response was generated primarily by the two presynaptic mechanisms.
The results indicate that, within a single neuron, different strategies are used within distinct
dendritic arbors to accomplish the same neural computation.

## Overview

Taylor and Vaney (2002) address a central unresolved question in retinal neuroscience: is direction
selectivity in On-Off DSGCs a property of upstream circuitry (presynaptic mechanisms) or of local
synaptic interactions within the DSGC's own dendrites (postsynaptic mechanisms)? Prior work by the
same group (Taylor et al., 2000) had concluded that direction selectivity was primarily
postsynaptic, based on experiments with high-chloride intracellular solutions and balanced total
conductance in both directions. A contemporaneous turtle study (Borg-Graham, 2001) reached the
opposite conclusion. This paper re-examines the rabbit DSGC using a more refined conductance
decomposition to resolve the discrepancy.

Using whole-cell voltage-clamp recordings from 28 On-Off DSGCs in whole-mount rabbit retina, the
authors measure total synaptic conductance while stepping holding potential across a range of -100
to +20 mV. From current-voltage (I-V) relations sampled every 10 ms during light stimuli, they
extract the time-varying reversal potential Vr(t) and total conductance gT(t), then decompose these
into excitatory ge(t) and inhibitory gi(t) components. The key finding is that three distinct
asymmetries jointly generate directional responses: stronger preferred-direction excitation
(presynaptic), stronger null-direction inhibition (presynaptic), and spatially delayed
null-direction inhibition in the off-arbor (postsynaptic). The on- and off-dendritic subarbors
employ different mixtures of these mechanisms despite producing equivalent directional tuning at the
spike output level.

The paper provides the first quantitative dissection of E/I conductance ratios across preferred and
null directions in the rabbit DSGC, demonstrating that the two subarbors are functionally distinct.
The off-dendrites exhibit a 160 um spatial offset of inhibition relative to excitation, consistent
with shunting inhibition operating locally within the electrotonically distributed off-arbor. The
on-dendrites rely predominantly on presynaptic asymmetries with no significant spatial offset of
inhibition. This result mandates that a compartmental model distinguish the on- and off-subarbors
both morphologically and in their synaptic input structure.

## Architecture, Models and Methods

**Preparation**: Dark-adapted New Zealand White rabbits. Whole-mount retina (10 x 10 mm section of
central retina) mounted photoreceptor-side down on CellTak-coated coverslip. Perfused with
oxygenated bicarbonate-buffered Ames medium at 33-37°C, pH 7.4, flow rate 2 ml/min.

**Cell identification**: On-Off DSGCs targeted by morphology (medium-large soma, crescent-shaped
nucleus). Preferred direction established by extracellular recording: bar swept in 12 directions at
30° intervals; direction of maximum spike count taken as preferred, opposite as null. On- and
off-preferred directions agreed within 0.3 +/- 11° (n = 11 cells).

**Whole-cell patch clamp**: Electrodes 4-8 MOhm. Intracellular solution: 110 mM Cs-gluconate or
CsCl, 10 mM NaCl, 5 mM Na-HEPES, 1 mM Cs-EGTA, 1 mM Na-ATP, 0.1 mM Na-GTP, 10 mM QX-314. Cs replaced
K to block voltage-gated K currents; QX-314 blocked Na channels and abolished spiking within 1-2
min. Liquid junction potential of 10 mV subtracted for Cs-gluconate.

**Light stimuli**: Barco Systems monitor (72 Hz). Focused through 40x (NA 0.75) or 20x (NA 0.35)
objectives. Bar width 250 um; bar velocity 800-1200 um/sec; contrast 0.3-1.0. Centred on soma.

**Conductance analysis**: Holding potentials stepped -100 to +20 mV in +15 mV increments (9 steps).
I-V relations constructed every 10 ms. Resting I-V (0.15 s pre-stimulus baseline) subtracted to
yield net light-evoked I-V. Slope = gT(t); x-intercept = Vr(t). Linear regression in Igor Pro;
non-linearities at extreme voltages handled by fitting over -70 to +10 mV or by selecting the 5-6
contiguous points yielding maximum gT.

**Conductance decomposition**: Assumed DSGC is isopotential, conductances linear, two types only
(excitatory Ve, inhibitory Vi). Ve = 0 mV (adjusted upward if Vr exceeded 0 mV); Vi = -65 mV
(adjusted downward if Vr fell below -65 mV). Assignment to nearest 5 mV. Formulae: gi(t) =
gT(t)(Vr(t) - Ve)/(Vi - Ve); ge(t) = gT(t)(Vr(t) - Vi)/(Ve - Vi). In-sample reversal potentials: Ve
= 4.3 +/- 5.5 mV; Vi = -65.7 +/- 2.3 mV (n = 16 cells).

**Spatial offset estimation**: dT = timing difference between inhibitory conductance peaks in
preferred minus null directions; dX = v x dT / 2 (v = stimulus speed).

**Direction-selectivity index**: D = |sum(ri x unit_vector_i)| / sum(ri); range 0 to 1.

**Sample size**: 28 DSGCs for total conductance; 16 cells (comparable on- and off-response size) for
full E/I decomposition.

## Results

* Total integrated null/preferred conductance ratio: **118 +/- 23%** (n = 28 cells) — slightly but
  consistently larger in null direction, indicating a net presynaptic imbalance.
* On-response preferred/null Ge ratio: **1.66 +/- 0.48** (excitation stronger in preferred).
* Off-response preferred/null Ge ratio: **1.36 +/- 0.13** (one outlier excluded; 1.54 +/- 0.73 with
  outlier).
* On-response null/preferred Gi ratio: **3.31 +/- 2.15** (null inhibition more than 3x stronger).
* Off-response null/preferred Gi ratio: **1.40 +/- 0.31**.
* On-response total directional conductance GD (preferred/null): **0.70 +/- 0.16**.
* Off-response total directional conductance GD (preferred/null): **0.94 +/- 0.18** (near unity).
* Spatial offset of off-inhibition: **dX = 160 +/- 56 um** (null relative to preferred) — the only
  offset significantly different from zero.
* Independent estimate from E-I timing within a direction: **148 +/- 48 um** (closely agrees).
* Spatial offsets for on-inhibition and on/off-excitation: not significantly different from zero.
* Directional-selectivity index D: on = **0.57 +/- 0.08**; off = **0.55 +/- 0.12** — identical.
* On- and off-preferred directions agreed to within **0.3 +/- 11°** (n = 11 cells).
* High-chloride experiments (5 cells): Vi shifted from **-63 +/- 7 mV** to **-28 +/- 5 mV** after
  2-6 min dialysis without changing total integrated conductance, confirming inhibitory synapses act
  directly on DSGC dendrites.

## Innovations

### First Quantitative E/I Decomposition in Rabbit On-Off DSGCs

This is the first study to separate time-resolved excitatory and inhibitory conductance components
at 10 ms resolution across full I-V sweeps in rabbit On-Off DSGCs. All previous rabbit work used
extracellular or pharmacological methods that could not isolate individual conductance components.
The decomposition enabled direct comparison of E/I balance in preferred vs null directions within
the same cell at every time point.

### Three-Mechanism Framework for Retinal Direction Selectivity

The paper identifies three co-existing and partially separable mechanisms: (1) presynaptic
excitatory asymmetry, (2) presynaptic inhibitory asymmetry, (3) postsynaptic spatially-offset
shunting inhibition in the off-subarbor. The finding that all three can operate simultaneously, and
that their relative contributions vary widely across cells (Fig. 5C scatter), resolves the
presynaptic vs postsynaptic debate by showing the question is a false dichotomy.

### On/Off Subarbor Dissociation Within a Single Neuron

Showing that the on- and off-dendritic subarbors of the same cell use different mechanism mixtures
to achieve the same directional spike output is a novel organisational principle. The off-arbor
exhibits a 160 um postsynaptic spatial offset absent from the on-arbor; the on-arbor shows far
stronger presynaptic inhibitory asymmetry (Gi ~3.3x) than the off-arbor (~1.4x).

### Reconciliation of Conflicting Prior Literature

The paper explicitly reconciles Taylor et al. (2000, postsynaptic conclusion) with Borg-Graham
(2001, presynaptic conclusion): the near-equal total conductance from 2000 arises because
directional excitation and directional inhibition cancel at the total conductance level. Both prior
studies were correct within their measurement scope.

## Main Ideas

* **Null-direction inhibition is ~3x stronger than preferred-direction inhibition in on-arbors** (Gi
  = 3.31x): any compartmental model should assign strongly asymmetric inhibitory conductance
  amplitudes to the on-subarbor, not equal amplitudes, when comparing preferred vs null stimuli.
* **Off-arbor inhibition is spatially offset by ~160 um**: this postsynaptic mechanism must be
  implemented as a spatial gradient of inhibitory synapses across the off-dendritic arbor; the 160
  um offset is approximately half a starburst amacrine cell radius and constrains synapse placement
  directly.
* **Push-pull E/I organisation**: directional excitation (preferred > null) and directional
  inhibition (null > preferred) co-exist; total conductance changes little between directions, so
  the model should not assume that stimulus direction alters total synaptic drive.
* **On- and off-subarbors are mechanistically distinct and must be modelled separately**: a
  single-arbor or single-compartment model cannot capture the data; implement separate on- and
  off-dendritic layers with independent inhibitory spatial offset profiles.
* **E/I ratio variability is large and should be a free parameter in optimisation**: the broad
  spread of Ge and Gi ratios across cells (Fig. 5C) precludes fixing these to mean values; treat E/I
  asymmetry as a tunable parameter bounded by the reported means and standard deviations.

## Summary

Taylor and Vaney (2002) ask whether direction selectivity in rabbit On-Off DSGCs arises from
directional upstream circuitry (presynaptic), from local postsynaptic E/I interactions in the
dendrites, or from a combination of both. The motivation is to resolve a contradiction between their
own 2000 study (postsynaptic conclusion) and a contemporaneous turtle study (presynaptic
conclusion), using a more complete conductance analysis in rabbit retina.

The approach is whole-cell voltage clamp in whole-mount retina with cesium-gluconate and QX-314 to
block intrinsic voltage-gated currents, enabling linear I-V relationships from which synaptic
conductance and reversal potential are extracted every 10 ms across 9 holding potentials. Excita-
tory and inhibitory conductances are isolated by two-component decomposition at assigned reversal
potentials (Ve ~0 mV, Vi ~-65 mV). Spatial offsets of inhibition are computed from inter- direction
timing differences of conductance peaks, assuming centred receptive fields.

Three mechanisms are found to coexist: preferred-direction excitation (**1.66x** on, **1.36x** off),
null-direction inhibition (**3.31x** on, **1.40x** off), and a **160 um** postsynaptic spatial
offset of off-inhibition with no equivalent in the on-arbor. The on-arbor relies chiefly on the two
presynaptic asymmetries; the off-arbor additionally uses the postsynaptic spatial offset. Total
conductance is nearly balanced across directions (~118% null/preferred), because directional
excitation and inhibition partially cancel. Both subarbors achieve identical directional tuning (D
~0.56) through distinct mechanisms, demonstrating within-neuron functional heterogeneity.

For this project, Taylor and Vaney (2002) is the primary empirical anchor for synaptic input
parameters in the DSGC compartmental model. The null/preferred Gi ratios (**3.31x** on, **1.40x**
off) constrain the inhibitory conductance asymmetry that the model must reproduce. The 160 um
inhibitory spatial offset in the off-subarbor specifies the spatial profile of inhibitory synapse
placement. The push-pull E/I structure implies both excitatory and inhibitory conductances must be
directionally modulated. The large cell-to-cell variability justifies treating E/I ratios as free
parameters in the optimisation, bounded by the reported means and standard deviations.
